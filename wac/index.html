The transformation and adoption layer for WAC deals.

I'm Mike McKeown, founder of Human-AI Systems. I work with WAC as a delivery partner: the senior practitioner who turns an AI strategy, advisory engagement or product deployment into adoption that actually lands inside the client. This page is a short brief on where I fit, when to bring me in, and how to engage me.

Where I fit

I carry the layer a WAC deal needs to land

WAC rarely sells a single thing. A typical deal pairs one of WAC's AI-native products, ContractIQ, Zehn, BoardHack and the rest of the portfolio, with the advisory and services wrapped around it. That combination is what wins the client, and it is what the client is buying into.

The part that most often decides whether they see real value is the layer around both the product and the services: redesigning workflows so the product fits how people actually work, building governance that enables rather than blocks, getting the organisation to adopt it, and proving the value in numbers.

That is my work. I sit inside a WAC engagement as the transformation and adoption lead, across both the product deployment and the services around it. It extends what WAC can credibly promise a client, without WAC having to carry that layer itself. WAC leads the strategy, the product and the build. I lead the organisational change that makes it stick.

Strategy and product get you the deal. Adoption is what makes the client renew and refer.

When to bring me in

Signals there is a role for me in a deal

A quick mental checklist. If a live opportunity shows any of these, there is probably a place for me in the shape of the engagement. Flag it early and we can work out what to say to the client.

  • The client wants strategy, governance or an operating model around a build, not just the build.
  • The deal is in a regulated sector where governance and evidence help win the sale: financial services, public sector, healthcare, legal.
  • There is real adoption or change risk: the technology will work, but the open question is whether people will use it.
  • The client needs training or capability-building alongside a product deployment.
  • There is an interim leadership gap: someone senior to own AI transformation for a defined period.
  • The client is early: they need an honest assessment and a roadmap before committing to a build.

How we keep it clean

Where WAC leads, and where I don't

I work as part of a WAC engagement, under the WAC relationship. WAC owns the client, the commercial arrangement and the product roadmap. I bring the transformation and adoption expertise into that.

I am not here to compete for the account or route around it. When I am in front of a client on a WAC deal, I am representing the joint proposition, not selling around it. That clarity is what makes it comfortable to put me in the room.

Beyond client deals

Advising WAC itself

Most of this page is about client work. There is a second way I can be useful, and that is to WAC directly: on the products, the technology, and how they go to market.

I am glad to be a sounding board here. If Andrew or the team want a quick view on a product decision, a technical trade-off, or how to take something to market, that kind of occasional, ad hoc input comes with the friendship and the partnership. Ask away, and I will give you an honest answer.

It becomes something different when the input turns structured or ongoing: shaping product strategy over time, working across the roadmap, building out go-to-market, or taking a defined advisory seat. That is real work and real value, and at that point it belongs in a proper arrangement, whether an advisory fee or an equity stake. I would welcome that conversation whenever it is useful, and it is easy to set up.

The simple line: ad hoc advice comes with the partnership. A standing advisory role is a commercial engagement, paid or equity.
How to engage me

Bringing me into a deal

The simplest path is to talk to me early. Once you spot a fit, a short call is usually enough to work out the shape of my involvement and what to say to the client.
