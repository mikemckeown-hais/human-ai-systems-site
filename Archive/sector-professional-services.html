Professional Services & Technology

AI changes the economics of knowledge work. The question is how to capture that value without compromising quality or client trust.

Professional services firms are under competitive pressure to adopt AI. The ones doing it well are redesigning how work actually happens — not just adding AI tools on top of existing workflows.

The challenge in professional services

Professional services is knowledge-intensive work. The economics of that work — how much time gets billed, at what rate, with what ratio of junior to senior staff — depend fundamentally on how efficiently knowledge workers can operate. AI changes those economics. But deploying it properly requires more than just licensing a tool.

Knowledge worker productivity is the competitive advantage. AI can improve it materially, but only if the workflows are redesigned around where AI actually helps. Client confidentiality adds another layer — what happens to client data when it enters an AI model? Some firms are cautious enough to exclude client data entirely; others are building governance that lets them use it safely.

Quality assurance and professional liability are harder questions in the AI era. AI-assisted work needs quality controls that existing processes weren't designed for. Professionals who define themselves by their expertise may resist AI adoption because they see it as a threat rather than augmentation. And clients are asking whether their advisors are using AI, and if so, how.

Where the opportunity is

AI creates value in professional services in three overlapping ways. The most successful programmes integrate all three.

Delivery workflow redesign

Identifying which parts of client work can be AI-assisted and redesigning the workflow to capture that value whilst preserving quality checkpoints and accountability. This is the core of most professional services AI programmes.

Knowledge management and research

Synthesis, precedent search, brief preparation, literature review. High value, lower risk, often the right starting point for firms that haven't yet resolved client data questions.

Operating model change

When AI is embedded into delivery, the economics of the firm change. Pricing, staffing ratios, quality review processes, training requirements all need to be rethought. This is the harder and more valuable work.

Technology companies

For technology companies, the AI transformation is both internal and strategic. The internal questions are about productivity — engineering, support, knowledge management. How do you help engineers write, review and ship code more efficiently? How do you scale customer support without proportional headcount growth? How do you make knowledge searchable and actionable?

The strategic questions are harder. What are the implications of AI for your products and your competitive position? Are you building products that will be displaced by AI-native competitors? Are you integrating AI capability into your product in a way that creates real differentiation for customers?

The Verimatrix programme is the reference point: an AI transformation of an engineering organisation that changed how code was written, reviewed, and shipped. The productivity gains were real. The revenue impact was real. The governance framework that the board approved — defining what AI could and couldn't do, how decisions were made, how quality was maintained — became a model for how engineering leadership thinks about AI adoption.

Consulting and advisory firms

The AI transformation of a consulting firm is also a transformation of its business model. That's why it's harder and why it creates more value when it works.

Firms that redesign their delivery model around AI can serve clients more efficiently and at higher quality. A team can do more client work in the same time. That changes what you can charge, who you can serve, and how you scale. It also changes the staffing model — you may need fewer junior staff, but you need the ones you have to be better at judgement and client management.

Firms that don't redesign will face pressure from competitors that have. The AI-enabled competitor can undercut you on price, or serve more complex clients at the same price with higher quality. Or both.

The challenge is that redesign takes time and leadership conviction. You have to be willing to change how you do work, how you price work, and how you hire and develop people. But the firms that do it successfully find that AI adoption changes their competitive position fundamentally.
