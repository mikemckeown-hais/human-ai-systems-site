Financial Services

Financial services AI adoption has to be governed from day one.

The regulatory environment — FCA, model risk frameworks, Consumer Duty — means you can't retrofit governance after the fact. The organisations getting AI right in financial services are the ones that designed the governance in before they deployed anything.

What makes financial services different

Financial services operates within a framework that most other sectors don't have to navigate. That framework exists for good reason — it protects consumers and maintains systemic stability. But it means that AI adoption in financial services has to be fundamentally different from AI adoption elsewhere.

The FCA, PRA, and Consumer Duty regulations create hard constraints on what you can deploy and how you can deploy it. Model risk requirements mean that AI decisions need to be auditable, explainable, and defensible under regulatory scrutiny. Your internal risk and compliance functions will need to approve anything deployed in a customer-facing or decision-making context — and rightly so.

Meanwhile, fintech and AI-native challengers are adopting AI faster than incumbent firms. Legacy technology debt affects data quality, which affects model quality. And most financial services organisations haven't yet hired people who understand both the AI and the regulatory dimensions.

"The question for most financial services organisations isn't whether to adopt AI. It's how to adopt it in a way that satisfies regulators, manages model risk, and actually changes how work gets done."

Where AI creates value in financial services

AI creates value in financial services in three distinct areas. Where you start depends on your regulatory constraints, your data quality, and what governance questions you need to resolve first.

Internal productivity first

Back-office operations, compliance document review, reporting, knowledge management. Lower regulatory risk, faster to pilot, significant efficiency gains. This is where most successful financial services AI programmes begin.

Decision support workflows

Underwriting, credit assessment, claims, advice — AI as a decision support tool with human sign-off preserved. Requires careful workflow design and governance to satisfy model risk requirements.

Customer operations

Communication, routing, self-service. Requires Consumer Duty compliance, fairness testing, and clear accountability. Higher governance complexity but significant potential.

How the services apply in financial services

Each of our core services is designed to address specific challenges in financial services AI adoption.

AI Adoption Radar

The governance risk assessment component of a Radar is particularly valuable in financial services — it maps which workflows have regulatory implications before any pilot commitment is made. Most organisations find the honest answer is that they're further from deployment-ready than they thought, and the Radar tells them specifically why and what to resolve.

Pilot Programmes

Piloting in financial services means compliance is designed in from the start — not added as a checklist at the end. The pilot blueprint defines governance controls, model risk oversight, and human sign-off requirements alongside the workflow design. That makes it easier to scale and easier to get past your risk and compliance function.

Scale & Operating Model

Embedding AI into a financial services operating model means defining human-AI accountability structures that regulators can understand, building governance review cycles into normal operations, and ensuring the workforce knows what it is and isn't appropriate to delegate to AI.

Fractional AI Leadership

A number of financial services organisations at the early stages of AI adoption benefit from senior AI leadership that can engage credibly with the board and with regulators — without the cost of a permanent hire. This is particularly relevant when the AI programme is at the stage where board-level reporting and regulatory dialogue are becoming necessary.
